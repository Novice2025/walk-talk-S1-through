Part 1 ¬∑ The Expressions

What do "talk someone through" and "walk someone through" mean?

The phrases "talk someone through" and "walk someone through" are both dynamic verbs used to describe the act of guiding someone step-by-step through a process, explanation, or demonstration. They go beyond a simple "explain" by implying a more active, detailed, and often interactive approach to ensure the other person fully understands or can perform a task.

While "explain" might simply involve stating facts or giving information, "talk someone through" and "walk someone through" convey a commitment to ensuring comprehension. You are literally "talking" or "walking" alongside them, making sure they follow each stage. This is particularly valuable in professional settings where clarity, accuracy, and shared understanding are paramount.

The subtle difference between the two often lies in the context: "Talk someone through" is frequently used for verbal explanations, concepts, or documents, where you're guiding them with words. For example, "I'll talk you through the new marketing strategy." "Walk someone through" often implies a more physical or visual demonstration, like guiding someone through a software interface, a physical process, or a sequence of actions. For instance, "I'll walk you through the steps to submit your expense report online." However, in many business contexts, they are used interchangeably to mean a thorough, guided explanation.

Think of it as "explicar detalhadamente," "guiar passo a passo," or "demonstrar o processo" em portugu√™s. While "explain" is fine, these phrases add a layer of active guidance and thoroughness that is highly valued in professional settings.

Part 2 ¬∑ When & how to use them

Professional contexts where Brazilians need these verbs.

In Portuguese, it's common to say "vou te explicar" (I will explain to you) or "vamos discutir os passos" (let's discuss the steps). In English, using "talk someone through" and "walk someone through" elevates this communication. They demonstrate that you're not just providing information, but actively guiding the person through the process, ensuring they grasp every detail. This shows a higher level of engagement and commitment to their understanding.

These phrases are particularly powerful because they imply a structured, methodical approach to sharing information. They suggest you're breaking down complexity into manageable parts, making it easier for the recipient to follow along and absorb the content. This is a significant upgrade from simply "explaining," which can sometimes feel less collaborative or less comprehensive.

Use ‚Äútalk someone through‚Äù or ‚Äúwalk someone through‚Äù when you want to:

Offer a Detailed, Guided Explanation: Instead of merely saying "I will explain the report," you can say "I will walk you through the report." This indicates that you will present each section, elaborate on every data point, and address questions along the way. It's a more active and engaging way to ensure comprehensive understanding, moving beyond a superficial overview.

Demonstrate a Process or System: When you need to show how something works, such as a new software application, a complex procedure, or a workflow, "walk someone through" is the perfect choice. You are literally "walking" with the person through each step, often visually or interactively, ensuring they can replicate the actions themselves. This is far more effective than just verbally "explaining" the steps.

Ensure Comprehension in Complex Situations: When information is dense, technical, or critical, using these expressions highlights your commitment to ensuring the listener not only hears the information but truly understands it. It's a proactive way to prevent misunderstandings, align expectations, and confirm that everyone is on the same page, especially when dealing with sensitive or high-stakes topics.

Build Trust and Collaboration: By offering to guide someone through a topic, you demonstrate patience, helpfulness, and a collaborative spirit. This approach is invaluable when onboarding new team members, training colleagues, or presenting intricate ideas to stakeholders. It positions you as a supportive expert rather than just a lecturer.

The Professional "Brazilian Way" of Explaining: For Brazilian professionals, who often value clarity, personal connection, and ensuring everyone is included, these expressions are the English equivalent of saying "vamos sentar e eu te mostro direitinho" (let's sit down and I'll show you properly) or "vou te dar uma aula sobre isso" (I'll give you a lesson on this), but in a more concise and formal business English manner. They convey the idea that you are there to help the person navigate the information, not just to deliver it. This makes your communication more impactful and culturally attuned.
